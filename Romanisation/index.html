McCune-Reischauer Romanisation

The McCune-Reischauer Romanisation of Korean was created in 1937 by George M. McCune, a history professor at the University of California, Berkeley, and Edwin O. Reischauer, a professor at Harvard University and a leading scholar in the history and culture of East Asia. The system of romanisation does not attempt to transliterate Korean, but rather represent the phonetic pronunciation.

Revised Romanisation

The Revised Romanisation of Korean was developed by the National Academy of the Korean Language from 1995 and was published by South Korea's Ministry of Culture and Tourism in 2000. The system is a modification of the older McCune-Reischauer system, removing the use of diacritics and apostrophes, with the aim of making romanisation of Korean more consistent, and making it compatible with the plain ASCII text of internet domain names.

The system is now the official romanisation system in South Korea.

Simplified Romanisation

The Simplified Romanisation of Korean was designed by me (yes, hello - I'm the author and the person who made this website). The problem with both the McCune-Reischauer and Revised systems is that to someone who has never studied Korean - and perhaps never intends to - they are quite unintuitive. The McCune-Reischauer Romanisation is a good representation of Korean, but it uses the characters ŏ and ŭ, which most non-Korean speakers don't understand, and which will often get changed to o and u. Similarly, the apostrophes used in the McCune-Reischauer system also get omitted. The Revised Romanisation of Korean is an adaptation of the McCune-Reischauer to try and solve both of those problems, but it ends up creating others. Many non-Korean speakers find the use of the digraphs eo and eu very confusing, leading to the pronunciation of words such as 서울 as 'see-owl'.

This simplified romanisation is designed to make pronouncing Korean words as easy as possible for people who haven't studied Korean at all. It's designed for English speakers, and takes advantage of our natural understanding of vowels in English to help with describing the pronunciation of Korean (for example, ㅜ in this system is written as oo, which most native English speakers will pronounce like MR u). Accuracy is lost through this system, because pronunciation of English letters is heavily dependent on context and accent, but to a certain extent accuracy is lost in all romanisation systems of Korean.

Yale Romanisation

The Yale Romanisation of Korean was developed by Samuel Martin, a professor of Far Eastern linguistics at Yale University. It is the standard system of romanisation used in linguistics, and it places an emphasis on showing a word's morphophonemic structure.

To the casual reader of Korean, the Yale Romanisation looks baffling, so it's unlikely to be needed by most people, but it's been included here for completeness.
